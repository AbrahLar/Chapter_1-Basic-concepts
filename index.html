Basic concepts

An electric circuit is an interconnection of electrical elements.

Charge and current

Charge is an electrical property of the atomic particles of which mater consists, mesuared in coulumbs \((\pu{C})\).
The following points should be noted about electric charge:

  1. We know that the charge \(\pu{e}\) on an electron is negative \(\pu{1 e} \approx \pu{-1.602 \times 10^{-19} C}\). In \(\pu{-1 C}\) of charge, there are approximately \(6.242 \times 10^{18}\) electrons.
  2. The only charges that occur in nature are integral multiples of the electron charge.
  3. The law of conservation of charge states that charge can neither be created nor destroyed, only transferred.

Electric current is the time rate of change of charge, mesuered in amperes \((\pu{A}\)).
Mathematically the relationship between current, charge and time is: $$ i \overset{\Delta}{=} \frac{\mathrm{d} q}{\mathrm{d} t} $$ The charge transferred between time \(t_{0}\) and \(t\) is obtained by integrating the current: $$ Q \overset{\Delta}{=} \int _{t_{0}} ^{t} i \ \mathrm{d} t $$ Where:
\(i\) is the current \((\pu{A})\).
\(q\) is the charge \((\pu{C})\).
\(t\) and \(t_{0}\) are a specific times \((\pu{s})\)

It is conventional to take the current flow as the movement of positive charges. That is, opposite to the flow of negative charges.

Voltage

Voltage (or potential difference) is the energy required to move a unit charge through an element, measured in volts \((\pu{V})\). $$ v_{ab} \overset{\Delta}{=} \frac{\mathrm{d} w}{\mathrm{d} q} $$ Where:
\(v_{ab}\) is the voltage between the points \(a\) and \(b\) \((\pu{V})\).
\(w\) is the energy requiered \((\pu{J})\).
\(q\) is the charge \((\pu{C})\).

The potential at point \(a\) with respect to point \(b\) is \(v_{ab}\). In general: $$ v_{ab} = -v_{ba} $$

Image 4

Like electric current, a constant voltage is called a dc voltage \((V)\), whereas a sinusoidally time-varying voltage is called an ac voltage \((v)\).

Power and energy

Power is the time rate of expending or absorbing energy, measured in watts \((\mathrm{W})\). $$ p \overset{\Delta}{=} \frac{\mathrm{d} w}{\mathrm{d} t} $$ Where:
\(p\) is the power \((\pu{W})\).
\(w\) is energy \((\pu{J})\).
\(t\) is the time \((\pu{s})\).
Or $$ p = vi $$ If \(p > 0\), power is being absorbed by the element. If, on the other hand, \(p < 0\), power is being supplied by the element.
The sum of power in a circuit, at any instant of time, must be zero. $$ \sum p = 0 $$

Energy is the capacity to do work, measured in joules \((\pu{J})\).
The energy absorbed or supplied by an element from time \(t_{0}\) to time \(t\) is: $$ w = \int _{t_{0}} ^{t} p \ \mathrm{d} t $$ The electric power utility companies measure energy in watt-hours \((\pu{W*h})\). \(\pu{1 W*h = \pu{3,600 J}}\).

Circuit elements

An active element is capable of generating energy while a passive element is not.
An ideal independent source is an active element that provides aspecified voltage or current that is completely independent of other circuit elements.

Image 6

An ideal dependent (or controlled) source is an active element in which the source quantity is controlled by another voltage or current.
